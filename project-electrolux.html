The Problem

Electrolux's operational reporting in LATAM was fragmented. Teams across manufacturing, logistics, and commercial used inconsistent dashboard structures, different visual languages, and varying levels of data clarity. There was no unified standard that could serve both on-the-floor teams and executive leadership from the same visual framework.

Dojo Smart Ways was contracted to build the operational dashboard layer. I was responsible for designing the visual identity and construction standard for everything that would be built on top of it.

The Constraint

Power BI is not a design tool. It has strict limitations on layout, component customization, and interaction patterns. Every design decision had to be evaluated not only for clarity and hierarchy, but for whether it was actually achievable in the Power BI environment we were delivering into.

I also came into Power BI with zero prior experience. I learned the tool in one week. By week two, I was contributing to live client projects. By the end of the engagement, I had designed and documented a system that could be handed off to any team and replicated consistently.

How it happened

From LATAM to global standard.

01

Business Understanding Sessions

Before touching a design file, I ran Business Understanding sessions with Data Analysts and Data Engineers. I needed to understand the data structure, the KPIs that mattered, and the decisions each dashboard was meant to support. Design without this context produces pretty screens that miss the point.

02

Visual Identity & Construction Standard

I defined the visual language for Electrolux's dashboard ecosystem: color system for data encoding, typography hierarchy, chart selection principles, KPI card structure, and layout grid. Everything was designed to work within Power BI's constraints while still feeling intentional and executive-ready.

03

LATAM Validation

The standard was first deployed across LATAM operations. Multiple functional teams used the system. Feedback was incorporated. The framework proved that it could scale across different data types and operational contexts without breaking down visually or informationally.

04

Global Approval

The LATAM results were presented to Electrolux's global board. The standard was approved and adopted company-wide. What started as a regional engagement became the design language for Electrolux's operational reporting globally.

Design Principles

Clarity over decoration.

Data First

Every visual element exists to serve comprehension. Color encodes meaning, not aesthetics. Chart types were chosen based on the cognitive task required, not based on what looks interesting. If a simpler chart tells the story better, the simpler chart wins.

Constraint as Design Material

Power BI's limitations shaped every decision. Rather than working around constraints, I worked within them deliberately: the system was designed to be reproducible by any team member without needing custom code or workarounds that could break across versions.

Executive and Operational

The same framework needed to work at two levels: an executive summary for leadership and an operational view for floor teams. The visual hierarchy was designed so both audiences could orient themselves immediately, without needing a guide or training.
